Deadlocks & starvation

Two failure modes, opposite in character. A deadlock is loud and total — every thread frozen, nothing progresses, forever. Starvation is quiet and selective — the system hums along beautifully while one thread waits and waits and burns out. This course covers the theory that makes deadlock preventable by construction (Coffman's four conditions, lock ordering), the reason fairness is a separate problem requiring separate machinery, and exactly how both land in Codexion — which is Dining Philosophers with three constraints Dijkstra never imagined.

Chapter 01

Coffman's conditions

1.1  What a deadlock actually is

Why this exists A deadlock is a set of threads, each waiting for a resource held by another thread in the same set, such that none can ever proceed. Nothing crashes. No error is returned. The program simply stops making progress, forever, while looking perfectly healthy from the outside. In 1971, Coffman, Elphick, and Shoshani identified four conditions that must all hold simultaneously for a deadlock to be possible — which immediately gives you four places to attack it.

1.2  The four conditions

ConditionMeaningIn Codexion
Mutual exclusionA resource is held exclusively — only one thread at a timeA dongle can be held by exactly one coder. Non-negotiable: it's the whole point.
Hold and waitA thread holds one resource while waiting for anotherA coder holds its left dongle while waiting for its right.
No preemptionA resource can't be forcibly taken; only the holder releases itYou can't yank a dongle out of a compiling coder's hand.
Circular waitA cycle exists: T1 waits on T2 waits on … waits on T1Every coder grabs left first: 1 waits on 2 waits on 3 … waits on 1.
The leverage this gives you All four must hold at once. Break any single one and deadlock becomes impossible — not unlikely, impossible. So deadlock prevention isn't about clever heuristics or hoping; it's about picking which of the four you're going to structurally eliminate, and being able to name it.

1.3  Which one can you actually break?

Work through them for Codexion:

  • Mutual exclusion — can't break it. The subject requires that a dongle not be duplicated. Removing exclusivity is the bug you're being graded against.
  • No preemption — can't reasonably break it. Forcibly taking a dongle from a mid-compile coder would corrupt the simulation's semantics.
  • Hold and wait — breakable. Require a coder to acquire both dongles atomically or neither: check both under one mutex, take both or wait. This is a legitimate, common solution.
  • Circular wait — breakable. Impose a global ordering on dongle acquisition so no cycle can form (Chapter 2).
Connect to Codexion Your README must contain a "Blocking cases handled" section describing "deadlock prevention and Coffman's conditions" — the subject names them explicitly. The strong answer isn't "I avoided deadlock"; it's "I break circular wait by imposing a global acquisition order" or "I break hold-and-wait by acquiring both dongles atomically under a single mutex," naming the condition and the mechanism.

Chapter 02

Circular wait & lock ordering

2.1  How a cycle forms

Why this exists Circular wait is the condition that arises by accident, from perfectly reasonable-looking local code. Nobody writes "create a cycle." Each coder just does the obvious thing — take my left dongle, then my right — and the cycle emerges from the geometry, invisible from any single thread's point of view.
The classic circular wait — five locally-correct decisions producing a globally fatal cycle.

Note the timing. If coders grab dongles at staggered times, one usually gets both and the cycle never closes. The deadlock only manifests when all five happen to hold their left dongle simultaneously — which is exactly why this bug can hide through hundreds of test runs and then strike during your evaluation.

2.2  Lock ordering: the general solution

The rule Assign every lockable resource a total order (any consistent one — by address, by index, by name). Require that any thread acquiring multiple locks acquires them in increasing order. A cycle is then mathematically impossible: a cycle would require some thread holding a higher-numbered lock while waiting for a lower-numbered one, which the rule forbids.

Applied to Codexion, the fix is famously small:

// Instead of always left-then-right:
struct dongle *first  = (coder->left->id < coder->right->id) ? coder->left  : coder->right;
struct dongle *second = (coder->left->id < coder->right->id) ? coder->right : coder->left;
take(first);
take(second);

Now coder 5 (whose left is dongle 5 and right is dongle 1) takes dongle 1 first — reversing its order relative to everyone else. That one asymmetry breaks the ring. The classic equivalent formulation: "odd-numbered coders take left first, even-numbered take right first."

2.3  Helgrind detects lock-order violations

Helgrind (Course 9) records the order in which each thread acquires locks and flags inconsistencies across threads — even if no deadlock actually occurred on that run. This is enormously valuable: it catches the latent cycle that would have deadlocked only under an unlucky interleaving you didn't happen to hit.

==12345== Thread #3: lock order "0x5A0 before 0x5B0" violated
==12345== Observed (incorrect) order is: acquisition of lock at 0x5B0
==12345==    at 0x109440: take_dongle (codexion.c:102)
==12345==  followed by a later acquisition of lock at 0x5A0
Connect to Codexion The evaluation scale is explicit: "If any of these tools reports a data race or a lock-ordering deadlock, tick the appropriate flag" — meaning 0. Helgrind will find your latent cycle whether or not it fires during the defense. Consistent lock ordering isn't defensive style here; it's a graded requirement with an automated detector pointed at it.

Chapter 03

Dining Philosophers

3.1  The original problem

Why this exists Dijkstra posed this in 1965: five philosophers sit around a table, each alternating between thinking and eating. Between each pair is a single fork. Eating requires both adjacent forks. The problem is deliberately minimal — no I/O, no networks, no complexity — so that the concurrency issues stand completely exposed. It has been the canonical teaching problem for deadlock, starvation, and resource arbitration ever since.

Codexion is Dining Philosophers with renamed pieces:

Dijkstra's versionCodexion
PhilosopherCoder (a thread)
ForkUSB dongle (a mutex)
Eating (needs 2 forks)Compiling (needs 2 dongles)
ThinkingDebugging, then refactoring
Starvation (dies)Burnout

3.2  The classic solutions

SolutionCoffman condition brokenVerdict for Codexion
Resource hierarchy — number the forks, always take lower firstCircular waitExcellent. Simple, provable, no extra state.
Arbitrator/waiter — a central authority grants permission to eatHold and waitNatural fit — your heap-based scheduler is the arbitrator.
Limit concurrency — allow at most N−1 philosophers to try at onceCircular wait (pigeonhole: someone always gets both)Works, but needs a semaphore — not in the allowed function list.
Atomic both-or-neither — take both forks under one lock or waitHold and waitClean and idiomatic with a condition variable.
Chandy/Misra — forks are dirty/clean, passed on requestCircular waitElegant but far more machinery than needed here.

3.3  Where Codexion diverges

The interesting part Codexion is not just Dining Philosophers. The subject adds three constraints that push it well past the textbook version:

  • Mandated arbitration policy — not merely "avoid deadlock" but "grant dongles in fifo or edf order." Dijkstra's version has no fairness policy requirement at all.
  • Dongle cooldown — a released dongle is unavailable for dongle_cooldown ms. No classical formulation has this; it creates a window where a dongle is neither held nor available.
  • Hard deadlines — time_to_burnout makes this a real-time problem, not just a liveness problem. "Eventually gets a fork" is insufficient; it must be soon enough.
Connect to Codexion — a likely defense question "Isn't this just Dining Philosophers?" The strong answer: "Yes, structurally — coders are philosophers, dongles are forks. But it adds a mandated arbitration policy (fifo/edf), a cooldown that makes a released dongle temporarily unavailable to anyone, and hard burnout deadlines that turn it from a liveness problem into a real-time scheduling problem. The classic resource-hierarchy solution prevents deadlock but doesn't satisfy any of those three."

Chapter 04

Starvation

4.1  Deadlock vs. starvation

The distinction that matters Deadlock: nobody progresses. The system is frozen; every thread in the cycle is stuck. Starvation: the system progresses fine — throughput may even look great — but one specific thread never gets served. Deadlock is loud and total. Starvation is quiet and selective, which makes it substantially harder to notice.
DeadlockStarvation
System throughputZeroPossibly excellent
Who's affectedEveryone in the cycleOne or a few unlucky threads
Resolves itself?NeverPossibly, by luck — which makes it intermittent
Codexion symptomProgram hangs, no more log linesOther coders compile happily; one burns out

4.2  How starvation arises

Three common mechanisms, all present in this problem space:

  • No fairness in the primitive. A raw pthread_mutex_t makes no ordering promise (Course 5, Chapter 4). A thread can lose the race repeatedly and indefinitely. Nothing in POSIX forbids it.
  • Barging. A newly-arriving thread that never waited can grab the lock the instant it's released, beating a thread that has been sleeping on it for a long time. The sleeper needs a wake + reschedule; the barger just runs a CAS.
  • Structural disadvantage. In Dining Philosophers, if both your neighbours eat frequently and are never both idle at the same moment, you never eat — even though the system is perfectly deadlock-free and the neighbours are following the rules.
The uncomfortable truth Preventing deadlock does not prevent starvation. A resource-hierarchy solution to Dining Philosophers is provably deadlock-free — and still permits a philosopher to starve indefinitely. They are different problems requiring different mechanisms.

4.3  Starvation in Codexion is a graded failure

Connect to Codexion The subject states: "The program must guarantee liveness: no coder should be starved of dongles and burn out under edf scheduling, provided the parameters are feasible." That's starvation, named directly, as a pass/fail criterion. And the evaluation scale's benchmark ./codexion 5 2000 200 200 200 10 0 edf is precisely a starvation test: the parameters are comfortably feasible (a 600ms cycle against a 2000ms burnout window), so any burnout means your arbitration is starving someone. There's no ambiguity to argue about — the parameters make feasibility a settled question.

Chapter 05

Fairness

5.1  Degrees of fairness

"Fair" is not one property. It's a spectrum, and being precise about which rung you're on is exactly what a defense question probes:

GuaranteeMeaningProvided by
NoneA thread may never be servedRaw pthread_mutex_t
Starvation-freedomEvery waiter is served eventuallyA FIFO queue
Bounded waitingServed after at most N othersA FIFO queue (N = queue length)
Deadline-awareServed in time to meet a deadline, if feasibleEDF
Why FIFO isn't the top rung FIFO guarantees you'll be served after at most N−1 others. But "eventually, in bounded time" is not the same as "in time." If your burnout deadline arrives while four coders are ahead of you in a perfectly fair queue, FIFO's fairness didn't save you. Deadline-awareness is a strictly stronger property — that's the entire argument for EDF.

5.2  FIFO vs. EDF, as fairness policies

Consider two coders waiting for a freed dongle:

  • Coder A requested at t=100, deadline at t=900 (plenty of time)
  • Coder B requested at t=150, deadline at t=260 (about to burn out)

FIFO serves A — it asked first, and that's the rule. B burns out. FIFO was perfectly fair by its own definition and produced a failure. EDF serves B, then A, and both survive. This is the exact scenario the subject's Medium benchmark cases are designed to surface by cranking dongle_cooldown to 800ms and forcing coders to queue.

EDF isn't strictly better — be precise Under overload (parameters where not everything can be met), EDF degrades badly: it chases the most urgent deadline, misses it anyway, then chases the next, potentially missing everything — a phenomenon called domino failure. FIFO under overload at least completes some requests fully. EDF's optimality guarantee holds specifically for feasible task sets — which is precisely why the subject writes "provided the parameters are feasible" rather than promising liveness unconditionally.

5.3  Where fairness lives in your code

The load-bearing insight from Course 5 and Course 6, restated because it's the design's spine:

ComponentResponsibilityCan it provide fairness?
pthread_mutex_tProtect shared state's integrityNo — POSIX guarantees no ordering
pthread_cond_tSleep and wake efficientlyNo — wakes an arbitrary waiter
Your heapOrder requests by fifo or edfYes — this is the only place fairness exists
Connect to Codexion This table is the answer to "why does the subject make me implement a heap?" Neither primitive it gives you can order anything. The heap is where the entire fairness requirement is satisfied — the mutex and condition variable are just the machinery that lets the heap's decision be enforced safely and efficiently.

Chapter 06

Codexion examples

6.1  The single-coder case

The evaluation scale's very first benchmark: ./codexion 1 800 200 200 200 10 0 fifo — one coder, one dongle. Compiling needs two. This coder can never compile and MUST burn out around t=800.

Why this test is first It checks two things at once. First, that your burnout detection actually fires within 10ms of the deadline. Second — and this is what it's really hunting — that you don't deadlock, crash, or hang in the degenerate case. A naive "take left, take right" implementation with one dongle may block forever on the second acquisition and never print the burnout line at all. Your monitor thread must remain responsive even while a coder is permanently blocked.

6.2  The cooldown trap

Codexion adds something Dining Philosophers never had: a released dongle is unavailable for dongle_cooldown ms. This creates a third state.

Dongle stateCan a coder take it?
FreeYes
HeldNo — someone's compiling with it
Cooling downNo — released, but not yet available
Why cooldown is a starvation amplifier Cooldown removes dongles from circulation for a fixed window, shrinking total availability. With ./codexion 5 3000 200 200 200 10 800 fifo, each dongle is unavailable for 800ms after every release — coders queue deeply, and any unfairness in your arbitration compounds. This is exactly why the scale pairs this test with the edf variant: the queueing is deliberately deep enough that grant order becomes observable in the logs.

Note also that a cooling dongle must not be granted early even to the coder the heap says is next. Your wait predicate needs all three: dongle is not held, cooldown has elapsed, and I am at the head of the queue.

6.3  The infeasible-by-design case

./codexion 5 500 200 200 200 10 0 fifo — one full cycle is 200+200+200 = 600ms, but time_to_burnout is 500ms. No scheduling policy, however brilliant, can save this. A coder must burn out around t=500.

Connect to Codexion — what's actually being tested This isn't testing your scheduler; it's testing your reporting. The scale requires: the burnout line must be the LAST line printed, and it must appear within 10ms of the deadline. That means your monitor detects burnout, immediately signals every thread to stop, and no coder manages to squeeze out another log line afterward. Getting the shutdown ordering right — under a mutex, with the stop flag checked before every print — is the whole exercise here. Course 10 covers the design.
The README checklist Your "Blocking cases handled" section should name, concretely: which Coffman condition you break and how; how you prevent starvation (the heap's ordering); how cooldown is enforced without granting early; how burnout is detected within 10ms; and how log serialization prevents interleaving. Those five map exactly onto what the scale checks.
